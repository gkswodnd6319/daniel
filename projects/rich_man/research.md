## 1. How FX Trading Works

### 1.1 What Is the Forex Market?

The foreign exchange market (forex or FX) is the largest and most liquid financial market in the world. As of 2022, the Bank for International Settlements (BIS) Triennial Survey reported average daily trading volume of approximately **$7.5 trillion**. To put that in perspective, the combined daily volume of all global stock markets is roughly $200-300 billion — forex dwarfs every other market.

**Key structural characteristics:**

- **Decentralized (OTC):** Unlike the Korean Stock Exchange (KRX) or the New York Stock Exchange, there is no single central exchange for forex. Trading occurs Over-The-Counter (OTC) through a global electronic network of banks, brokers, institutions, and individual traders. There is no single "price" — rates vary slightly across venues.
- **24-hour market:** Forex operates continuously from Monday morning in Sydney to Friday evening in New York (roughly Sunday 22:00 UTC to Friday 22:00 UTC). This means 24 hours a day, 5 days a week.
- **Interbank market:** The core of the forex market is the interbank market, where major global banks trade with each other. The "retail" forex market that individual traders access is a derivative of this interbank market.

**Market participants (in order of influence):**

| Participant | Role | Approximate Share |
|---|---|---|
| Central banks (BOK, Fed, ECB, BOJ) | Monetary policy, FX intervention, reserve management | Significant but variable |
| Commercial banks (Citi, JPMorgan, Deutsche Bank, UBS) | Market-making, proprietary trading, client flow | ~40-50% of volume |
| Institutional investors (pension funds, mutual funds, hedge funds) | Portfolio allocation, speculation, hedging | ~25-30% |
| Corporations (Samsung, Hyundai, etc.) | Hedging export/import revenues | ~5-10% |
| Retail traders (individuals) | Speculation | ~5-7% |
| Sovereign wealth funds | Reserve diversification | Variable |

For Korean traders, this means: when you trade USD/KRW, you are participating in a market shaped primarily by Korean exporters hedging dollar revenues (Samsung, SK Hynix, Hyundai), the Bank of Korea managing exchange rate stability, and international hedge funds speculating on Korean macro conditions.

### 1.2 Currency Pairs Explained

All forex trading involves pairs — you are always simultaneously buying one currency and selling another.

**Notation:** A currency pair is written as **BASE/QUOTE** (or BASE/COUNTER).

- **USD/KRW = 1,350** means: 1 USD costs 1,350 KRW
- The **base currency** (USD) is on the left — it is the currency you are buying or selling
- The **quote currency** (KRW) is on the right — it tells you how much of this currency you need to buy/sell one unit of the base

**If you "buy" USD/KRW at 1,350:** You are buying USD and selling KRW. If the rate rises to 1,400, you profit.

**If you "sell" USD/KRW at 1,350:** You are selling USD and buying KRW. If the rate falls to 1,300, you profit.

#### Types of Currency Pairs

**Major Pairs** (most liquid, tightest spreads — all include USD):

| Pair | Name | Avg Daily Volume |
|---|---|---|
| EUR/USD | "Euro" or "Fiber" | ~$800B+ |
| USD/JPY | "Dollar-Yen" or "Gopher" | ~$600B+ |
| GBP/USD | "Cable" | ~$400B+ |
| USD/CHF | "Swissy" | ~$150B+ |
| AUD/USD | "Aussie" | ~$200B+ |
| USD/CAD | "Loonie" | ~$200B+ |
| NZD/USD | "Kiwi" | ~$50B+ |

**Minor/Cross Pairs** (no USD involvement):

- EUR/GBP, EUR/JPY, GBP/JPY, AUD/NZD, EUR/CHF, etc.
- Still liquid, but wider spreads than majors

**Exotic Pairs** (one major + one emerging market currency):

- **USD/KRW** — this is classified as an exotic pair
- USD/TRY (Turkish Lira), USD/ZAR (South African Rand), USD/MXN (Mexican Peso), USD/THB (Thai Baht), USD/INR (Indian Rupee)
- Exotics have **wider spreads, lower liquidity, and higher volatility** than majors

**Important for Korean traders:** USD/KRW is an exotic pair. This means:
- Spreads are significantly wider than EUR/USD (10-50 pips vs 0.1-1 pip)
- Not all international brokers offer it
- Much of the KRW trading occurs in the **NDF (Non-Deliverable Forward) market** offshore, not spot
- Liquidity is concentrated during Seoul trading hours (09:00-15:30 KST)

### 1.3 How Exchange Rates Are Determined

Exchange rates are determined by supply and demand, but many factors drive that supply and demand:

#### Interest Rate Differentials

The single most important driver of exchange rates. Money flows to where it earns the highest risk-adjusted return.

- **Example:** In 2022-2023, the US Federal Reserve raised rates aggressively to 5.25-5.50%, while the Bank of Korea raised to 3.50%. This 175+ basis point differential favored the USD, pushing USD/KRW from ~1,200 to ~1,350+.
- **Carry trade:** Traders borrow in low-interest-rate currencies (JPY at 0-0.5%) and invest in higher-yielding ones (USD at 5%+). This creates persistent demand for the higher-yielding currency.

#### Trade Balance / Current Account

- South Korea is a major exporter (semiconductors, automobiles, ships, electronics). When Korean exports are strong, foreign buyers need KRW to pay Korean companies (or Korean companies convert USD to KRW), which strengthens the won.
- When Korean imports rise (especially oil — Korea imports nearly 100% of its crude), Korea needs more USD, weakening the won.
- Korea's trade balance has historically been positive, supporting KRW over the long term.

#### Macroeconomic Data

Key data releases that move USD/KRW:

| Data Release | Country | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Non-Farm Payrolls (NFP) | US | Strong jobs = Fed hawkish = USD strength |
| CPI (Consumer Price Index) | US / Korea | Inflation drives rate decisions |
| FOMC Rate Decision | US | Directly moves USD against all currencies |
| BOK Rate Decision | Korea | Directly moves KRW |
| GDP Growth | Both | Broad economic health |
| PMI (Purchasing Managers Index) | Both | Leading indicator of economic activity |
| Korean Export Data (1st of each month) | Korea | Early indicator of trade balance and tech demand |
| Semiconductor Shipments | Korea | Given the sector's weight in Korean exports |

#### Market Sentiment / Risk Appetite

- KRW is considered a **risk-sensitive** or **pro-cyclical** currency. When global risk appetite is high (stocks up, VIX low), KRW tends to strengthen. When fear rises, KRW weakens.
- USD is considered a **safe haven.** During crises, money flows into USD, pushing USD/KRW higher.
- KRW is often used as a proxy for **emerging market risk** in Asia.

#### Capital Flows

- Foreign investment into Korean stocks (KOSPI) and bonds requires buying KRW, which strengthens it.
- When foreigners sell Korean assets (capital outflow), they convert KRW back to USD/EUR/JPY, weakening the won.
- The Korea Financial Supervisory Service tracks daily foreign net buying/selling.

#### Central Bank Intervention

- The Bank of Korea actively intervenes in the FX market to smooth excessive volatility. Korea maintains substantial foreign exchange reserves (~$410-420 billion) for this purpose.
- Intervention typically occurs when USD/KRW moves too fast in either direction, not to defend a specific level.

### 1.4 Bid/Ask Spread Explained

The **bid** is the price at which the market (or your broker) will buy the base currency from you (i.e., the price you get when selling). The **ask** (or offer) is the price at which the market will sell the base currency to you (i.e., the price you pay when buying).

**The ask is always higher than the bid.** The difference is the spread — it is the broker's/dealer's profit margin and your primary transaction cost.

#### Example: USD/KRW

```
Bid:  1,348.50
Ask:  1,349.50
Spread: 1.00 KRW (or about 10 pips if 1 pip = 0.10 KRW for this pair)
```

If you buy USD/KRW at the ask (1,349.50) and immediately sell at the bid (1,348.50), you lose 1.00 KRW per dollar. On a $10,000 trade, that is an instant cost of 10,000 KRW (~$7.40).

#### Example: EUR/USD

```
Bid:  1.08520
Ask:  1.08530
Spread: 0.00010 = 1.0 pip
```

Notice how much tighter this is. On a standard lot (100,000 EUR), a 1-pip spread costs you $10.

#### Spread Comparison by Pair

| Pair | Typical Retail Spread | Classification |
|---|---|---|
| EUR/USD | 0.1 - 1.0 pips | Major |
| USD/JPY | 0.2 - 1.0 pips | Major |
| GBP/USD | 0.5 - 2.0 pips | Major |
| USD/KRW | 5 - 50 pips (0.50 - 5.00 KRW) | Exotic |
| USD/TRY | 10 - 80 pips | Exotic |

Spreads widen during:
- Low liquidity periods (Asian session for EUR/USD, overnight for USD/KRW)
- High-volatility events (NFP release, BOK rate surprise, geopolitical shock)
- Market open/close transitions

### 1.5 Lot Sizes

Forex trades are measured in **lots.** A lot defines the size of your trade.

| Lot Type | Units of Base Currency | Example: USD/KRW at 1,350 |
|---|---|---|
| **Standard Lot** | 100,000 units | 100,000 USD = 135,000,000 KRW (~135 million won) |
| **Mini Lot** | 10,000 units | 10,000 USD = 13,500,000 KRW (~13.5 million won) |
| **Micro Lot** | 1,000 units | 1,000 USD = 1,350,000 KRW (~1.35 million won) |
| **Nano Lot** | 100 units | 100 USD = 135,000 KRW (~135,000 won) |

**Pip value by lot size (for USD/KRW, where 1 pip = 0.01 KRW):**

- Standard lot: 1 pip move = 100,000 x 0.01 = 1,000 KRW (~$0.74)
- Mini lot: 1 pip move = 10,000 x 0.01 = 100 KRW (~$0.074)

**Note:** For USD/KRW, "pip" conventions vary. Some platforms treat 1 pip = 0.01 KRW (e.g., 1,350.00 to 1,350.01). Others treat 1 pip = 0.10 KRW or even 1.00 KRW. Always check your broker's pip definition for this pair.

For major pairs like EUR/USD, 1 pip = 0.0001 (fourth decimal place). For JPY pairs, 1 pip = 0.01 (second decimal place).

### 1.6 Leverage and Margin

Leverage allows you to control a large position with a small amount of capital. It is expressed as a ratio.

**How it works:**

- **50:1 leverage** means you need only 1/50th (2%) of the trade value as margin
- **100:1 leverage** means you need only 1/100th (1%) of the trade value as margin
- **10:1 leverage** means you need 1/10th (10%) of the trade value as margin

#### Concrete Example: 50:1 Leverage on USD/KRW

You want to buy 1 standard lot (100,000 USD) of USD/KRW at 1,350.

- **Total position value:** 100,000 USD = 135,000,000 KRW
- **Required margin at 50:1:** 100,000 / 50 = **2,000 USD** (or 2,700,000 KRW)
- **You control $100,000 with only $2,000**

**Scenario A: USD/KRW rises from 1,350 to 1,365 (+1.11%)**
- Profit: 100,000 x (1,365 - 1,350) = 1,500,000 KRW (~$1,099)
- Return on margin: $1,099 / $2,000 = **+54.9% return**

**Scenario B: USD/KRW falls from 1,350 to 1,335 (-1.11%)**
- Loss: 100,000 x (1,335 - 1,350) = -1,500,000 KRW (~-$1,099)
- Return on margin: -$1,099 / $2,000 = **-54.9% loss**
- You have lost more than half your margin from just a 1.1% move

**Scenario C: USD/KRW falls from 1,350 to 1,323 (-2.0%)**
- Loss: 100,000 x (1,323 - 1,350) = -2,700,000 KRW (~-$2,041)
- **Your entire $2,000 margin is wiped out. You receive a margin call or are automatically liquidated.**

#### Margin Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Used Margin** | The amount of capital locked up in open positions |
| **Free Margin** | Account equity minus used margin — available to open new trades |
| **Margin Level** | (Equity / Used Margin) x 100% — below 100% is danger zone |
| **Margin Call** | A warning that your margin level has fallen below a threshold (often 100%) |
| **Stop-Out Level** | The margin level at which positions are forcibly closed (often 50%) |

#### Leverage by Jurisdiction

| Jurisdiction | Max Retail Leverage |
|---|---|
| South Korea (domestic brokers) | Varies; FX margin trading restrictions apply |
| Japan | 25:1 (regulated since 2011) |
| EU / UK | 30:1 (major pairs), 20:1 (minors/exotics) |
| Australia | 30:1 (since 2021) |
| United States | 50:1 (major pairs), 20:1 (minors) |
| Offshore brokers | Up to 500:1 or even 1000:1 (extremely dangerous) |

### 1.7 Long vs Short Positions

**Going Long (Buying):**
- You buy the base currency, expecting it to appreciate relative to the quote currency
- **Long USD/KRW at 1,350:** You believe USD will strengthen / KRW will weaken. You profit if USD/KRW rises to 1,400.
- Equivalent to: buying dollars, selling won

**Going Short (Selling):**
- You sell the base currency, expecting it to depreciate relative to the quote currency
- **Short USD/KRW at 1,350:** You believe USD will weaken / KRW will strengthen. You profit if USD/KRW falls to 1,300.
- Equivalent to: selling dollars, buying won

**Critical concept:** In forex, going short is just as easy as going long. There is no "uptick rule" or borrowing requirement like in stock short-selling. Every forex trade is simultaneously long one currency and short another.

**Korean exporter analogy:**
- Samsung earns USD from chip sales abroad. They need to convert USD to KRW to pay Korean employees. Samsung is effectively "short USD/KRW" — they benefit from a stronger won (lower USD/KRW).
- Korean Air imports jet fuel priced in USD. They are effectively "long USD/KRW" — they suffer when the won weakens.

### 1.8 Order Types

#### Market Order
- Executes immediately at the current best available price
- **Use when:** You want to enter/exit NOW, and a few pips of slippage are acceptable
- **Risk:** You may get a worse price than displayed, especially in fast markets

#### Limit Order
- An order to buy below or sell above the current market price
- **Buy Limit:** Place a buy order below current price. "I want to buy USD/KRW, but only if it dips to 1,340."
- **Sell Limit:** Place a sell order above current price. "I want to sell USD/KRW, but only if it rises to 1,370."
- **Use when:** You want a specific entry price and are willing to wait

#### Stop Order (Stop Entry)
- An order to buy above or sell below the current market price (used for breakout strategies)
- **Buy Stop:** "Buy USD/KRW if it breaks above 1,360" (entry on upward breakout)
- **Sell Stop:** "Sell USD/KRW if it breaks below 1,330" (entry on downward breakout)

#### Stop-Loss Order
- A protective order that closes your position at a specified loss level
- **Example:** You buy USD/KRW at 1,350 with a stop-loss at 1,340. If the rate drops to 1,340, your position is automatically closed, limiting your loss to 10 KRW per dollar.
- **On a mini lot (10,000 USD):** Loss capped at 10,000 x 10 = 100,000 KRW (~$74)
- **Essential for risk management.** Never trade without a stop-loss.

#### Take-Profit Order
- Automatically closes your position when a target profit level is reached
- **Example:** Buy USD/KRW at 1,350 with a take-profit at 1,375. Your position closes automatically at +25 KRW per dollar if the rate reaches 1,375.

#### Trailing Stop
- A dynamic stop-loss that moves with the price in your favor but stays fixed if price moves against you
- **Example:** Buy USD/KRW at 1,350 with a 10-KRW trailing stop. If price rises to 1,370, your stop moves to 1,360. If price then falls, you are stopped out at 1,360 (locking in a 10 KRW profit per dollar rather than your original entry).

#### OCO (One-Cancels-Other)
- Two orders are placed simultaneously; when one triggers, the other is automatically cancelled
- Common setup: a take-profit and a stop-loss bracket around an open position

### 1.9 Trading Sessions (with KST Times)

The forex market operates 24 hours, but activity is not uniform. There are four major sessions:

| Session | Local Time | KST (UTC+9) | Key Currencies |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sydney** | 07:00 - 16:00 AEDT | 05:00 - 14:00 KST | AUD, NZD |
| **Tokyo** | 09:00 - 18:00 JST | 09:00 - 18:00 KST | JPY, KRW, SGD |
| **London** | 08:00 - 17:00 GMT | 17:00 - 02:00 KST (next day) | EUR, GBP, CHF |
| **New York** | 08:00 - 17:00 EST | 22:00 - 07:00 KST (next day) | USD, CAD |

**Seoul FX Market Hours:**
- The onshore USD/KRW spot market in Seoul operates from **09:00 to 15:30 KST** on business days.
- The closing fix rate is set at **15:30 KST** — this is the benchmark rate (called the "서울외국환중개 매매기준율" or Seoul Money Brokerage reference rate).
- After 15:30 KST, USD/KRW continues to trade in the offshore NDF market.

**Key overlaps (highest liquidity, best for trading):**

- **Tokyo-London overlap: 17:00 - 18:00 KST** — moderate increase in activity
- **London-New York overlap: 22:00 - 02:00 KST** — **highest liquidity period for major pairs** (EUR/USD, GBP/USD). Most of global forex volume occurs here.
- **Seoul session: 09:00 - 15:30 KST** — **best time for USD/KRW trading.** This is when the onshore market is active, spreads are tightest, and official fixing occurs.

**For Korean traders:**
- If you trade EUR/USD or GBP/USD, the most active period is late evening to early morning KST (22:00 - 02:00).
- If you trade USD/KRW, focus on 09:00 - 15:30 KST when the Seoul onshore market is open.
- If you trade USD/JPY, the Tokyo session (09:00 - 18:00 KST) offers good liquidity.

### 1.10 How a Trade Actually Executes — Step by Step

Let us walk through a complete trade: **Buying 1 mini lot of USD/KRW at 1,350.**

**Step 1: Analysis and Decision**
You analyze that US CPI came in hotter than expected, meaning the Fed may stay hawkish. You decide USD will strengthen against KRW. You want to buy USD/KRW.

**Step 2: Place the Order**
On your trading platform, you:
- Select pair: USD/KRW
- Select direction: Buy
- Select size: 1 mini lot (10,000 USD)
- Set stop-loss: 1,340 (10 KRW below entry = max loss of 100,000 KRW)
- Set take-profit: 1,370 (20 KRW above entry = target profit of 200,000 KRW)
- Click "Buy" or "Place Order"

**Step 3: Order Routing**
Your order travels from your trading platform to your broker's server. The broker either:
- **A-books it:** Routes to a liquidity provider (bank) in the interbank market
- **B-books it:** Acts as counterparty themselves (market-making)
- **Hybrid:** Some of each, depending on the trade size and client profile

**Step 4: Execution**
The order is filled at 1,350.30 (ask price; the bid was 1,349.80, so the spread is 0.50 KRW).

**Step 5: Position is Open**
- You are now long 10,000 USD against KRW
- Margin locked: 10,000 / 50 (assuming 50:1 leverage) = 200 USD = ~270,000 KRW
- Unrealized P&L: Currently -0.30 KRW per dollar (you entered at the ask, and mark-to-market is at the mid) = -3,000 KRW. This is the cost of the spread.

**Step 6: Position Management**
Over the next few hours, USD/KRW rises to 1,358. Your unrealized profit: (1,358 - 1,350.30) x 10,000 = 77,000 KRW. You decide to move your stop-loss to 1,352 (breakeven + small profit) to protect gains.

**Step 7: Exit**
USD/KRW hits 1,370. Your take-profit order triggers. Position is closed at 1,370.
- Gross profit: (1,370 - 1,350.30) x 10,000 = **197,000 KRW** (~$146)
- Minus: any overnight swap fee if held overnight, any commission

**Step 8: Settlement**
In retail forex, there is no physical delivery of currency. The profit/loss is credited/debited to your account immediately. If you traded an actual deliverable spot contract (T+2 settlement), the currencies would exchange in 2 business days, but retail platforms handle this automatically via rollover.

## 2. Risks of FX Trading

### 2.1 Market Risk / Volatility Risk

Exchange rates can move rapidly and unpredictably. For an exotic pair like USD/KRW, daily moves of 10-20 KRW (0.7-1.5%) are common, and during crisis periods, moves of 50-100+ KRW in a single day have occurred.

#### Historical Examples of Extreme KRW Moves

**1997 Asian Financial Crisis:**
- USD/KRW went from approximately **850 in early 1997** to **1,962 in December 1997** — a **130% devaluation** in months
- Korea's foreign reserves were nearly depleted; the country required a $58.4 billion IMF bailout
- Many Korean businesses with unhedged USD debt went bankrupt overnight
- This event remains seared into Korean collective financial memory

**2008 Global Financial Crisis:**
- USD/KRW surged from ~**1,000 in March 2008** to ~**1,513 in March 2009**
- A 50%+ depreciation of the won in 12 months
- The BOK intervened heavily, spending tens of billions in reserves
- Lehman Brothers' collapse caused a global dollar funding squeeze that crushed emerging market currencies

**2020 COVID-19 Pandemic:**
- USD/KRW spiked from ~**1,160 in January 2020** to ~**1,296 in March 2020** in just weeks
- A ~12% move as global panic drove USD demand
- The BOK cut rates to a record low of 0.50% and established a $60 billion swap line with the US Federal Reserve
- KRW recovered relatively quickly as Korea's COVID response was seen as effective

**2022 USD Strength / Fed Tightening:**
- USD/KRW moved from ~**1,190 in January 2022** to ~**1,445 in September 2022** (a 13-year high)
- Driven by aggressive Fed rate hikes while BOK hiked more slowly
- Global risk-off sentiment and a surging dollar index (DXY) pressured all EM currencies

**Flash crash potential:** On October 2, 2016, GBP/USD crashed 6% in about 2 minutes during the Asian session (thin liquidity). Similar flash crashes can affect USD/KRW during low-liquidity hours (after Seoul close, before London open).

### 2.2 Leverage Risk

Leverage is the primary reason retail FX traders lose money. It amplifies both gains AND losses proportionally.

**The math of leverage-induced ruin:**

| Leverage | Move Against You | Loss on Margin |
|---|---|---|
| 10:1 | 1% | 10% of margin lost |
| 10:1 | 10% | **100% — margin wiped out** |
| 50:1 | 1% | 50% of margin lost |
| 50:1 | 2% | **100% — margin wiped out** |
| 100:1 | 0.5% | 50% of margin lost |
| 100:1 | 1% | **100% — margin wiped out** |
| 500:1 | 0.2% | **100% — margin wiped out** |

**Real scenario:** You deposit 5,000,000 KRW (~$3,700) and trade with 50:1 leverage. You go long 1 standard lot of USD/KRW (100,000 USD) at 1,350.

- If USD/KRW drops just **27 KRW (2%)** to 1,323, your loss is 100,000 x 27 = 2,700,000 KRW. That is 54% of your entire account gone from a 2% move.
- If USD/KRW drops **50 KRW (3.7%)** to 1,300, your loss is 5,000,000 KRW. **Your entire account is wiped out.** In fact, with slippage, you could owe MORE than your deposit (negative balance).
- A 50 KRW move in USD/KRW is not unusual — it can happen within a week during volatile periods.

**Key lesson:** Just because you CAN use 50:1 leverage does not mean you SHOULD. Professional traders typically use 5:1 to 10:1 effective leverage. Many experienced traders use even less.

### 2.3 Liquidity Risk

Not all currency pairs or all times of day offer the same liquidity. Low liquidity means:
- Wider spreads (higher cost)
- Slippage on execution (getting filled at a worse price)
- Potential inability to exit a position at any reasonable price

**KRW-specific liquidity concerns:**
- USD/KRW is primarily liquid during Seoul market hours (09:00-15:30 KST)
- After Seoul close, liquidity drops dramatically in the spot market; NDF market takes over
- During Korean public holidays, onshore KRW trading is essentially shut down while offshore NDF trading continues — gaps can occur when Seoul reopens
- Major KRW liquidity providers: Korean banks (Hana, KB, Shinhan, Woori, NH), global banks with Seoul branches (Citi, JPMorgan, Standard Chartered)

### 2.4 Interest Rate Risk

Changes in interest rates (or expectations of changes) are the primary driver of medium-term currency trends.

- If you hold a position that benefits from a specific interest rate differential, an unexpected rate change can reverse your trade rapidly.
- **Example:** You are long USD/KRW expecting the interest rate differential to favor USD. If the BOK unexpectedly raises rates by 50bp while the Fed signals a pause, USD/KRW could drop 20-40 KRW in a single session.
- **Swap risk:** If you hold positions overnight, changes in interest rate differentials affect your daily swap costs/credits (covered in the costs section).

### 2.5 Country / Political Risk

This is especially relevant for KRW:

**North Korea:**
- Missile tests, nuclear tests, or military provocations cause immediate KRW weakness
- September 2017: During heightened North Korea tensions (ICBM tests, "fire and fury" rhetoric), USD/KRW spiked above 1,140 — a roughly 5% move over a few months
- The market has somewhat "desensitized" to North Korea risk over time, but a genuine military escalation would cause extreme KRW depreciation
- December 2024: Political instability following the martial law declaration briefly pushed USD/KRW above 1,470

**Domestic Korean politics:**
- Presidential impeachments (2004 Roh Moo-hyun, 2016 Park Geun-hye) caused temporary KRW volatility
- Policy changes around capital controls or tax rules can impact KRW demand

**Global geopolitics:**
- US-China trade wars directly affect Korea (a major trading partner with both)
- Russia-Ukraine conflict (2022) impacted Korea through energy prices and global risk sentiment
- Disruptions to global semiconductor supply chains have outsized impact on KRW

### 2.6 Counterparty Risk

Your broker could default, go bankrupt, or engage in fraud.

- **Regulated brokers** in major jurisdictions (UK FCA, US NFA/CFTC, Australia ASIC, Japan FSA) offer some protections (segregated client funds, compensation schemes)
- **Offshore/unregulated brokers** (common with very high leverage offerings) provide minimal protection. If they go under, your money may be gone.
- **Korean domestic FX trading:** If you trade through a Korean bank, counterparty risk is relatively low given the strength of Korean banking regulation.
- Always verify your broker's regulatory status.

### 2.7 Gap Risk

Forex gaps occur when the market opens at a significantly different price from the previous close. This happens:

- **Over weekends:** Forex closes Friday evening New York time (Saturday 07:00 KST) and reopens Sunday evening (Monday 07:00 KST). If a major event occurs over the weekend, the market can open at a very different price.
- **After holidays:** Korean market holidays can create gaps when Seoul reopens.
- **Example:** If USD/KRW closes at 1,350 on Friday and North Korea conducts a nuclear test on Saturday, the market might open at 1,370+ on Monday — your stop-loss at 1,340 would be meaningless because there was no price at 1,340 to trigger it. You would be stopped out at the opening price of 1,370 or worse.

**Stop-losses do NOT guarantee your maximum loss.** They guarantee execution at the best available price after the stop level is breached, which during a gap can be far from your stop price.

### 2.8 Psychological Risks

The most dangerous risks in FX trading are often internal:

**Overtrading:**
- Taking too many positions out of boredom or excitement
- Trading without a clear thesis or setup
- Increasing position sizes after winning streaks (overconfidence)

**Revenge Trading:**
- After a loss, immediately placing a larger trade to "win it back"
- This often leads to cascading losses
- The most common path to blowing up an account

**FOMO (Fear of Missing Out):**
- Entering a trade too late because "the move already started"
- Chasing price — buying after a big up move or selling after a big down move
- Usually results in entering at the worst possible time

**Fear of Loss / Premature Exit:**
- Closing winning trades too early because you are afraid the profit will disappear
- Holding losing trades too long because you "hope" it will come back
- The combination (cutting winners short, letting losers run) is mathematically devastating

**Confirmation Bias:**
- Only seeking information that supports your existing position
- Ignoring warning signs or contradictory data

### 2.9 Retail FX Trader Success Rates

The statistics are sobering:

- **US CFTC and NFA data** (required disclosure by US-regulated brokers): **Approximately 70-80% of retail FX traders lose money.** Some brokers report figures as high as 80-85% of accounts losing.
- **EU ESMA data** (European brokers must disclose): Similar figures, with most brokers showing 70-82% of retail accounts losing money.
- **Academic studies** (e.g., by Barber, Lee, Liu, and Odean, and by the French AMF) consistently find that:
  - The majority of retail FX traders are unprofitable
  - Traders who use higher leverage lose more
  - Frequent traders underperform infrequent traders
  - Most retail traders exhibit well-documented behavioral biases

**Why do most retail traders lose?**
1. Excessive leverage
2. No proper risk management
3. Emotional decision-making
4. Overtrading
5. Insufficient education and preparation
6. Trading costs (spreads, swaps) erode small edges
7. Competing against institutional players with superior information, speed, and capital

## 3. Costs Associated with FX Trading

### 3.1 Spreads

The spread is the difference between the bid and ask price and is typically the **largest cost** for retail FX traders.

#### Typical Spreads by Pair (Retail Broker)

| Pair | Typical Spread (Variable) | In Pip Value per Standard Lot |
|---|---|---|
| EUR/USD | 0.1 - 1.5 pips | $1 - $15 |
| USD/JPY | 0.2 - 1.5 pips | $1.30 - $10 |
| GBP/USD | 0.5 - 2.5 pips | $5 - $25 |
| AUD/USD | 0.4 - 2.0 pips | $4 - $20 |
| USD/KRW | 5 - 50+ pips | Variable; typically 50,000 - 500,000+ KRW |
| EUR/JPY | 0.5 - 3.0 pips | $3 - $20 |

**USD/KRW spread details:**
- Through Korean banks (for actual currency conversion): The spread can be 1-10 KRW above/below the market mid-rate. Banks typically charge a "환전우대" (exchange rate preferential) margin. Without any preferential rate, a bank might quote 1,340 / 1,360 (20 KRW spread).
- With preferential rates (90% 우대): The spread narrows significantly, perhaps to 1,349 / 1,351 (2 KRW spread).
- Through international forex brokers offering USD/KRW: 5-30 pip spreads typically.

**Fixed vs Variable spreads:**
- **Fixed spread** brokers guarantee a constant spread (e.g., always 2 pips on EUR/USD) but the spread is usually wider than the variable minimum
- **Variable spread** brokers offer tighter spreads during normal conditions but spreads widen during volatility and low liquidity

### 3.2 Commissions

Some brokers charge a separate commission per trade (in addition to or instead of a spread).

**Common commission structures:**

| Account Type | Spread | Commission |
|---|---|---|
| Standard (spread-only) | 1.0 - 2.0 pips | None |
| ECN / Raw Spread | 0.0 - 0.5 pips | $3 - $7 per standard lot per side |

**Example (ECN account):**
- You trade 1 standard lot of EUR/USD
- Spread: 0.2 pips = $2
- Commission: $3.50 per side x 2 (open + close) = $7.00
- **Total cost: $9.00** per round trip
- Compare to a standard account with a 1.2 pip spread: $12.00 per round trip. The ECN account is cheaper.

For USD/KRW through Korean banks, there is typically no explicit "commission" — the cost is embedded in the wide spread.

### 3.3 Swap / Rollover Fees

If you hold a forex position overnight (past the daily rollover time, typically 17:00 New York time = 07:00 KST next day), you pay or receive a **swap** based on the interest rate differential between the two currencies.

**How it works:**

When you go long (buy) a currency pair:
- You earn interest on the base currency you "hold"
- You pay interest on the quote currency you "borrowed"

When you go short (sell) a currency pair:
- You pay interest on the base currency you "borrowed"
- You earn interest on the quote currency you "hold"

The net difference (adjusted by the broker, who typically takes a markup) is your swap.

**Example: Long USD/KRW**

Suppose:
- US interest rate (Fed Funds): 5.25%
- Korean interest rate (BOK Base Rate): 3.50%
- Differential: +1.75% favoring USD

If you are long USD/KRW (long USD, short KRW):
- You earn ~5.25% on your USD position
- You pay ~3.50% on your KRW position
- **Net: you earn approximately +1.75% annualized** (before broker markup)
- This is called a **positive swap** — you earn money for holding overnight

If you are short USD/KRW:
- You pay ~5.25% on USD
- You earn ~3.50% on KRW
- **Net: you pay approximately -1.75% annualized** (plus broker markup)
- This is called a **negative swap** — it costs you money to hold overnight

**Daily swap calculation (approximate):**
- Position: 1 standard lot (100,000 USD) long USD/KRW
- Annual swap: 100,000 x 1.75% = $1,750
- Daily swap: $1,750 / 365 = ~$4.79 per day (credited to your account)
- After broker markup (they typically take 0.5-1.0%), your actual credit may be ~$2-3 per day

**Triple swap Wednesday:** Because forex settles T+2, a trade opened on Wednesday settles Friday. To account for the weekend (Saturday and Sunday), brokers charge/credit triple the normal swap on Wednesday night. This means the Wednesday overnight hold carries 3x the swap cost/credit.

### 3.4 Slippage

Slippage is the difference between the price you expected and the price at which your order actually executes.

**Types:**
- **Positive slippage:** You get a better price than requested (rare but possible)
- **Negative slippage:** You get a worse price than requested (more common)

**When slippage occurs:**
- During high volatility (news releases, market opens)
- During low liquidity (outside major session hours)
- With market orders (limit orders are immune to negative slippage by definition, but may not fill)
- On large position sizes relative to available liquidity

**USD/KRW slippage:**
- During Seoul trading hours, slippage on reasonable position sizes is minimal
- Outside Seoul hours (in the NDF market), slippage can be significant
- During major data releases (US NFP, Korean export data), slippage of 2-10 KRW is possible

If your trading account is denominated in one currency and you trade a pair in another, or if you deposit/withdraw in a different currency, you incur conversion costs.

**Bank conversion rates vs broker rates vs market rates:**

### 3.6 Tax Implications in Korea

**Important:** Tax laws change. Always consult a qualified Korean tax professional. The following is general information based on known rules.

#### Taxation of FX Trading Gains for Korean Residents

**Category: 기타소득 (Other Income) or 양도소득 (Capital Gains)**

FX margin trading profits through overseas brokers are generally classified under Korean tax law as follows:

- **Offshore FX trading gains:** Classified as overseas-derived income. Korean residents have a global income tax obligation — they must report and pay tax on worldwide income, including FX trading gains from international brokers.
- **Tax rate on FX gains:** Generally classified under 기타소득세 (other income tax) or potentially 양도소득세 (capital gains tax) depending on the specific structure. The applicable rate is typically **22% (including local surtax)** on net gains for most FX derivative transactions. Actual classification and rate may vary — seek professional advice.
- **Reporting:** Gains must be reported in the annual tax return (종합소득세 신고) filed by May of the following year. For overseas financial accounts exceeding 500 million KRW (approximately $370,000) at any point during the year, you must file a **해외금융계좌 신고** (Overseas Financial Account Report) with the National Tax Service by June 30.
- **Failure to report:** Penalties are severe. Failure to report overseas financial accounts can result in fines of up to 20% of the unreported amount, plus potential criminal penalties.

**FX gains from Korean bank currency exchange:**
- If you simply convert KRW to USD at a bank, hold the USD, and convert back later at a profit, this is not generally subject to capital gains tax (it is considered a basic financial transaction, not a "trading" activity). However, structured FX products or margin trading gains are taxable.

**FX gains from Korean bank FX-linked products:**
- FX-linked structured deposits (외화연계 정기예금), FX derivatives offered by Korean banks: These are typically subject to 배당소득세 or 이자소득세 (dividend/interest income tax) at 15.4% (including surtax), withheld at source.

### 3.8 Hidden Costs

**Requotes:**
- In fast-moving markets, your broker may reject your order at the displayed price and offer you a new (worse) price. This is a "requote."
- Common with market-maker (B-book) brokers, rare with ECN/STP brokers.

**Platform fees:**
- MetaTrader 4/5 is free from the broker, but some brokers charge for premium tools, advanced charting, or VPS hosting.
- Some Korean brokers charge for real-time quote feeds or advanced order types.

**Price manipulation / stop hunting:**
- Some disreputable brokers (typically unregulated) may manipulate their price feed to trigger client stop-losses. The price on their platform briefly spikes to hit your stop, then returns to normal. This is essentially theft and is one reason to use a regulated broker.

**Information asymmetry:**
- Brokers can see their clients' aggregate positions and stop-loss levels. While regulated brokers should not trade against this information, the incentive exists for B-book brokers.

## 4. FX Trading in Korea (한국에서의 외환거래)

### 4.1 Legal Framework

South Korea permits foreign exchange transactions but regulates them heavily. The primary legislation is:

- **외국환거래법 (Foreign Exchange Transactions Act / FETA)** — the core law governing all foreign exchange transactions by Korean residents and entities. Originally enacted in 1961, substantially revised in 1999 (post-Asian Crisis liberalization) and periodically updated.
- **외국환거래규정 (Foreign Exchange Transaction Regulations)** — detailed implementing regulations under FETA.
- The key regulators are the **기획재정부 (Ministry of Economy and Finance)**, the **한국은행 (Bank of Korea)**, and the **금융감독원 (Financial Supervisory Service / FSS)**.

**What Korean residents can legally do:**

1. **Buy/sell foreign currency** at banks for travel, study abroad, investment, etc. — generally permitted with documentation
2. **Maintain foreign currency accounts** at Korean banks (외화예금) — permitted
3. **Send money overseas** — permitted with reporting requirements above certain thresholds
4. **Invest in overseas securities** through Korean brokers — permitted (under 해외투자 rules)
5. **FX margin trading** — this is the complex area (see Section 4.5)

### 4.2 Korean FX Regulations (Key Rules)

#### Capital Flow Reporting Requirements

| Transaction Type | Reporting Threshold | Where to Report |
|---|---|---|
| Overseas remittance (해외송금) | Reporting to designated FX bank for amounts over $5,000 | Your bank |
| Payment for goods/services | Report to designated FX bank | Your bank |
| Capital transactions (investment) | Prior notification/approval may be required for large amounts | BOK or designated bank |
| Overseas financial account | Report if balance exceeds 500 million KRW at any point in the year | National Tax Service (by June 30) |
| Cash carrying across border | Must declare if carrying over $10,000 equivalent | Customs |

#### Key Regulatory Principles

- **Designated Foreign Exchange Bank (외국환은행):** Korean residents must generally conduct FX transactions through a "designated" Korean bank. You designate one bank for specific types of FX transactions.
- **Purpose documentation:** For large or non-routine FX transactions, you may need to provide documentation showing the purpose (e.g., tuition payment receipts for study abroad remittances, import invoices for trade payments).
- **Annual limits:** Individual overseas remittances of up to $50,000 per year for personal purposes are relatively straightforward. Amounts exceeding this may require additional documentation and reporting.
- **Overseas real estate investment:** Requires prior notification to the designated FX bank. There are specific rules and limits.

### 4.5 FX Margin Trading Regulations in Korea

This is a critical area for Korean residents interested in leveraged FX speculation.

**Current status:**

- **Domestic leveraged FX margin trading for retail investors is effectively restricted.** Korea does not have a well-developed domestic retail FX margin trading industry like Japan or the UK.
- Korean securities firms can offer certain derivative products, but full-featured leveraged FX margin trading accounts (like those offered by OANDA, IG, or forex.com internationally) are not commonly available through Korean-regulated entities for individual retail traders.
- **FX derivatives** (options, futures, forwards) are available through Korean banks and securities companies, but usually require:
  - Classification as a 전문투자자 (professional investor) or meeting specific income/asset thresholds
  - Evidence of hedging need or investment sophistication
  - Minimum investment amounts that are prohibitive for small retail traders

**The grey area of offshore brokers:**

Many Korean traders interested in leveraged FX trading use international (offshore) brokers. The regulatory position is:
- There is no explicit Korean law that criminalizes an individual for opening an account with an overseas broker
- However, the overseas broker is not licensed to solicit Korean customers, and doing so would violate Korean regulations on the broker's side
- The Korean trader retains all tax and reporting obligations
- If you have a dispute with an offshore broker, Korean regulators cannot help you
- The 금융감독원 (FSS) periodically warns about the risks of unregistered overseas FX platforms

**Bottom line:** Korean residents who want true leveraged FX margin trading typically need to use offshore brokers, accepting the regulatory and counterparty risks involved. For simple FX conversion or basic hedging, Korean banks are the regulated and safe option.

### 4.8 KRW as a Restricted Currency — The NDF Market

**Why KRW is special:**

The Korean won is **NOT a freely convertible currency.** Unlike USD, EUR, or JPY (which anyone in the world can freely hold and trade), KRW has restrictions:

- Non-residents cannot freely hold KRW accounts onshore without specific approval
- Offshore KRW trading is limited
- The KRW is classified as a "partially convertible" currency

This means that most international trading of KRW happens through **NDFs (Non-Deliverable Forwards)** rather than spot transactions.

#### What Is an NDF?

A Non-Deliverable Forward (NDF) is a forward contract on a currency that **settles in USD** (or another major currency) rather than in the restricted currency itself.

**How a USD/KRW NDF works:**

1. Two parties agree on a forward exchange rate for USD/KRW (e.g., 1,360 for a 1-month forward)
2. At settlement, no KRW actually changes hands
3. Instead, the difference between the agreed rate and the fixing rate (Seoul onshore closing rate at 15:30 KST) is calculated, and the net difference is paid **in USD**

**Example:**
- Party A buys a 1-month USD/KRW NDF at 1,360, notional $1 million
- At settlement, the fixing rate is 1,380
- USD/KRW went UP (KRW weakened), so Party A profits
- Profit: $1,000,000 x (1/1,360 - 1/1,380) = $1,000,000 x (0.0007353 - 0.0007246) = ~$10.67 per $1,000 notional = **~$10,672 USD** settled in cash

**Why NDFs matter for Korean traders:**

- The NDF market trades **24 hours** globally (London, New York, Singapore, Hong Kong), providing continuous USD/KRW pricing even when the Seoul onshore market is closed
- NDF rates influence (and are influenced by) the onshore spot rate
- When Seoul opens at 09:00 KST, the opening rate often reflects overnight NDF movements
- Major international banks, hedge funds, and real-money investors use the NDF market for KRW exposure
- Daily NDF volume in USD/KRW is estimated at $10-20 billion, making it one of the most active NDF markets globally

**Recent development:** Korea has been gradually moving toward greater KRW internationalization. In 2024, the BOK extended onshore FX trading hours and allowed more participation by overseas financial institutions, as part of efforts to get Korean bonds included in major global indices (e.g., FTSE World Government Bond Index).

### 4.9 Bank of Korea (BOK) Interventions

The BOK has a long history of intervening in the FX market to manage KRW volatility:

**How intervention works:**

- **To strengthen KRW (lower USD/KRW):** The BOK sells USD from its foreign exchange reserves and buys KRW. This increases USD supply and KRW demand, pushing the rate down.
- **To weaken KRW (raise USD/KRW):** The BOK buys USD and sells KRW. Less common, but occurs when KRW appreciation threatens export competitiveness.
- **Verbal intervention:** BOK officials make statements like "we are watching the FX market closely" or "there is excessive volatility" — this alone can influence trader behavior.
- **Coordinated intervention:** Sometimes conducted with the National Pension Service (NPS, 국민연금) or other state entities.

**Historical intervention patterns:**

| Period | Direction | Approximate Reserves Used | Context |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2008 GFC | Defending KRW (selling USD) | $60+ billion | USD/KRW surged past 1,500 |
| 2009-2012 | Slowing KRW appreciation (buying USD) | Reserves rebuilt to $310B+ | Post-crisis recovery, strong export growth |
| 2015-2016 | Defending KRW | Reserves drawn from ~$370B | China slowdown fears, capital outflows |
| 2020 COVID | Defending KRW, using Fed swap line | Swap line of up to $60B | Dollar funding crisis |
| 2022 | Defending KRW aggressively | Reserves fell from ~$463B to ~$416B | USD/KRW hit 1,445; multi-decade KRW weakness |

**Korea's foreign exchange reserves** (approximately $410-420 billion) are among the 9th largest globally, providing substantial firepower for intervention. However, reserves are not unlimited, and prolonged one-way pressure can exhaust them (as nearly happened in 1997).

**Trader implications:**
- Fighting the BOK is risky. If you are short KRW and the BOK aggressively defends it, you can suffer rapid losses.
- The BOK does not defend a specific level — it aims to smooth volatility. This means they may step in after a 20-30 KRW daily move, but may tolerate a gradual 100 KRW move over several months.
- Watch for verbal cues: statements from the Finance Minister or BOK Governor about "excessive volatility" or "one-sided moves" are pre-intervention warnings.

| 2024 | 1,280 → 1,470 range | US rate expectations, Korea political events |

#### 1997 Asian Financial Crisis — The Defining Moment

Before 1997, Korea operated a managed float regime, keeping USD/KRW around 850. When the crisis hit:

- Thai baht collapse in July 1997 triggered contagion across Asia
- Korean chaebols had massive short-term foreign debt (over $150 billion)
- Foreign reserves dropped to under $10 billion (usable)
- The government was forced to let the won float freely in December 1997
- USD/KRW went from 850 to nearly 2,000 in weeks
- Korea received a $58.4 billion IMF/World Bank rescue package
- The crisis led to fundamental restructuring of the Korean economy and financial system
- Korea repaid its IMF loans ahead of schedule by 2001

**Lesson for traders:** Extreme tail risks in KRW are not theoretical. A repeat of a 1997-scale crisis is highly unlikely given modern Korea's massive reserves, flexible exchange rate, and strong macro fundamentals, but the 2022 move to 1,445 showed that significant depreciation is always possible.

#### 2008 Global Financial Crisis

- Korean won was among the worst-performing Asian currencies in 2008
- USD/KRW rose from ~1,000 (March 2008) to ~1,513 (March 2009)
- Drivers: global dollar shortage, risk aversion, concerns about Korean bank external debt
- The BOK intervened aggressively and secured a $30 billion swap line with the Fed
- The KRW's vulnerability during this period led Korea to massively build up reserves in subsequent years

#### 2020 COVID-19

- USD/KRW spiked from ~1,160 to ~1,296 in March 2020 (global panic)
- The BOK activated its $60 billion swap line with the Fed
- KRW recovered relatively quickly (back below 1,200 by October 2020) because:
  - Korea's COVID response was effective (no lockdowns, strong testing)
  - Korean semiconductor exports boomed (work-from-home demand)
  - Global risk appetite recovered rapidly due to fiscal/monetary stimulus

#### 2022 Rate Divergence

- The most significant recent KRW move: from ~1,190 in January to ~1,445 in September 2022
- The Fed raised rates from 0.25% to 4.50% during 2022 alone; the BOK raised from 1.00% to 3.25%
- The widening rate differential made USD more attractive vs KRW
- Simultaneously, a global semiconductor downturn hit Korea's export revenues
- Korea ran its first trade deficit in years (driven by high energy import costs)
- The BOK intervened, burning through roughly $40-50 billion in reserves

### 5.2 Key Drivers of USD/KRW

Understanding what moves USD/KRW is essential. Here are the primary drivers, roughly in order of importance:

#### 1. US Federal Reserve Policy (Fed Funds Rate, Forward Guidance)

The single most important factor. When the Fed raises rates or signals hawkishness, USD strengthens globally and USD/KRW rises. Conversely, dovish Fed signals weaken USD and lower USD/KRW.

- **Correlation:** USD/KRW is highly correlated with the DXY (Dollar Index) — roughly 0.7-0.85 correlation over most periods.
- **Key events:** FOMC meetings (8 per year), Fed Chair press conferences, dot plot updates, Fed minutes.

#### 2. Bank of Korea (BOK) Policy

- **BOK rate decisions** (8 per year, announced at 10:00 KST on the decision day) directly impact KRW
- Surprise rate changes cause the biggest moves
- Rate differential with the US is the key — when the differential widens in favor of the US, USD/KRW tends to rise

#### 3. Korean Trade Balance / Semiconductor Cycle

- Korea's trade balance is heavily influenced by the **global semiconductor cycle**
- Semiconductors account for roughly **20% of Korea's total exports**
- When chip prices are high and demand is strong (e.g., 2021, 2024 AI boom), trade surplus expands, KRW strengthens
- When chip prices collapse (e.g., 2022-2023 memory downturn), trade surplus shrinks or turns to deficit, KRW weakens
- **Monthly export data** (released on the 1st of each month by the Ministry of Trade) is the earliest indicator

#### 4. Global Risk Sentiment

- KRW is a "risk-on" currency — it strengthens when global markets are bullish and weakens during risk-off episodes
- Key indicators to watch: VIX (fear index), S&P 500, global PMIs, EM capital flows
- In a "risk-off" event (geopolitical crisis, financial market turmoil), KRW typically underperforms because:
  - Foreign investors sell Korean stocks and bonds (capital outflow)
  - The USD strengthens as a safe haven
  - Korean export outlook darkens

#### 5. Foreign Investor Flows into Korean Assets

- Foreign investors hold significant positions in KOSPI stocks and Korean government bonds
- Net foreign buying of Korean stocks/bonds supports KRW
- Net foreign selling pressures KRW
- Data released daily by the Korea Exchange (KRX) and monthly by the BOK

#### 6. Oil Prices

- Korea imports nearly 100% of its crude oil
- Higher oil prices increase Korea's import bill, worsening the trade balance, weakening KRW
- Lower oil prices reduce the import bill, supporting KRW
- Correlation: USD/KRW tends to have a positive correlation with oil prices (higher oil = higher USD/KRW = weaker KRW)

### 5.3 Seasonal Patterns

While FX markets do not have strong seasonal patterns (compared to, say, equities), some tendencies have been observed in USD/KRW:

- **January-February:** Korean companies may repatriate overseas earnings from the previous fiscal year, creating KRW demand (potential USD/KRW downward pressure)
- **March-April:** Dividend season — foreign investors receiving dividends from Korean stocks may convert KRW to USD/EUR and repatriate, creating temporary KRW selling pressure
- **May-June:** Korean tax payment season; limited direct FX impact
- **September-December:** US budget/fiscal year dynamics; global portfolio rebalancing ahead of year-end
- **Lunar New Year / Chuseok periods:** Market liquidity drops significantly during Korean holidays

**Caution:** Seasonal patterns are weak and unreliable in FX. Do not trade solely based on seasonality.

### 5.4 USD/KRW Correlations with Other Assets

| Asset | Typical Correlation with USD/KRW | Logic |
|---|---|---|
| **DXY (Dollar Index)** | Strong positive (+0.7 to +0.85) | USD strength drives USD/KRW up |
| **KOSPI (Korean stock index)** | Moderate negative (-0.4 to -0.7) | Risk-on = KOSPI up, KRW strong (USD/KRW down) |
| **US 10-Year Treasury Yield** | Moderate positive (+0.3 to +0.6) | Higher US yields attract capital to USD |
| **VIX (Volatility Index)** | Moderate positive (+0.3 to +0.6) | Higher fear = risk-off = weaker KRW |
| **Oil (WTI/Brent)** | Weak positive (+0.1 to +0.4) | Higher oil worsens Korea's trade balance |
| **Copper** | Weak negative (-0.1 to -0.3) | Industrial metal demand = global growth = risk-on |
| **USD/JPY** | Moderate positive (+0.4 to +0.6) | Both are Asian currencies that weaken when USD surges |
| **USD/CNY** | Strong positive (+0.6 to +0.8) | KRW is heavily influenced by China; both move with USD |
| **Samsung Electronics stock** | Moderate negative (-0.3 to -0.5) | Samsung = proxy for Korean economy and exports |

**Key insight:** If you are long USD/KRW and simultaneously long KOSPI puts, you have a somewhat redundant position (both profit from "risk-off"). Understand correlations to avoid unintentional concentration.

### 5.5 The Role of Korean Exporters and Importers

Korean companies are major participants in the USD/KRW market:

**Exporters (Samsung, SK Hynix, Hyundai Motor, POSCO, LG, etc.):**
- Earn USD from overseas sales
- Need to convert USD to KRW to pay Korean employees, suppliers, and taxes
- Are natural KRW buyers / USD sellers
- Tend to hedge (sell USD forward) when USD/KRW is high — this creates resistance as USD/KRW rises
- Samsung Electronics alone generates $200+ billion in annual revenue, a substantial portion in USD
- Exporter hedging activity is most visible when USD/KRW reaches psychologically significant levels (1,300, 1,350, 1,400, etc.)

**Importers (Korean Air, refiners like SK Innovation, raw material buyers):**
- Pay USD for imports (oil, gas, raw materials, aircraft)
- Need to buy USD with KRW
- Are natural USD buyers / KRW sellers
- Tend to buy USD when USD/KRW is low — this creates support as USD/KRW falls

**Net effect:** Korea's trade balance determines the net direction of corporate FX flow. In trade surplus periods, exporters' USD selling dominates, supporting KRW. In trade deficit periods (rare for Korea, but occurred in 2022), the balance shifts.

### 5.6 How Korean Semiconductor Exports Affect KRW

Korea is the world's largest memory semiconductor producer (Samsung and SK Hynix together control roughly 70% of global DRAM and 50% of NAND flash).

**The semiconductor-KRW linkage:**

1. **Strong chip demand / high prices → trade surplus grows → KRW strengthens**
   - 2021: Memory prices surged, Korea's semiconductor exports hit record ~$128 billion, trade surplus was healthy, USD/KRW stayed below 1,200
   - 2024: AI-driven demand for HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) boosted semiconductor exports, supporting KRW

2. **Weak chip demand / low prices → trade surplus shrinks → KRW weakens**
   - 2022-2023: Memory downturn, prices fell 50%+, semiconductor exports dropped sharply, Korea ran monthly trade deficits, USD/KRW surged past 1,400

3. **Leading indicator:** Semiconductor export data (released monthly, with a preliminary figure on the 1st) is one of the most-watched Korean economic indicators for FX traders.

4. **Samsung/SK Hynix capex announcements:** Major investment plans signal confidence in future demand, indirectly supporting KRW through expected future export revenue.

**Practical insight:** Tracking global memory chip spot prices (DRAMeXchange/TrendForce) and monthly Korean semiconductor shipment data gives you an edge in forecasting medium-term USD/KRW direction.

## 6. Strategies for Beginners

### 6.1 Start with a Demo Account

Before risking real money, trade on a demo account for **at least 2-3 months**.

**What a demo account provides:**
- Real-time market data and charts
- Practice executing orders without financial risk
- Ability to test strategies in live market conditions
- Familiarization with the trading platform

**How to use a demo account effectively:**
- Treat it like real money. Do not take positions you would never take with real capital.
- Set a realistic starting balance that matches what you plan to deposit for real trading (do not practice with $100,000 on demo if you plan to deposit $1,000)
- Track your performance rigorously — win rate, average win vs average loss, maximum drawdown
- Only go live when you have demonstrated **consistent profitability** over at least 2-3 months on demo

**Warning:** Demo trading is psychologically different from live trading. The absence of real financial risk means emotions (fear, greed) are muted. Many traders who are profitable on demo struggle when real money is at stake. Start live trading with the smallest position sizes possible.

### 6.2 Risk Management

**The 1-2% Rule:**

Never risk more than **1-2% of your total account equity** on a single trade.

**Example:**
- Account balance: 5,000,000 KRW (~$3,700)
- Maximum risk per trade (2%): 100,000 KRW (~$74)
- If your stop-loss is 10 KRW on USD/KRW, maximum position size: 100,000 / 10 = 10,000 USD (1 mini lot)

**Why this works:** Even a string of 10 consecutive losses (which happens more often than you think) would only cost you 20% of your account. You survive to trade another day.

**Additional risk management rules:**

1. **Always use a stop-loss.** No exceptions.
2. **Never move your stop-loss further away** from your entry to "give the trade more room." This turns a controlled risk into an uncontrolled one.
3. **Risk-reward ratio:** Aim for at least 1:2 (risk $1 to make $2). This means your take-profit should be at least twice as far from entry as your stop-loss.
4. **Maximum open risk:** Total risk across all open positions should not exceed 5-6% of your account at any time.
5. **Correlation risk:** If you have 3 trades that are all "long USD" (e.g., long USD/KRW, short EUR/USD, short GBP/USD), you effectively have one concentrated bet. Count correlated positions together.

### 6.3 Position Sizing Calculator

Use this formula every time you place a trade:

```
Position Size = (Account Risk Amount) / (Stop-Loss Distance in Currency Units)

Where:
  Account Risk Amount = Account Balance x Risk Percentage (e.g., 2%)
  Stop-Loss Distance = |Entry Price - Stop-Loss Price|
```

**Worked example for USD/KRW:**

- Account balance: 10,000,000 KRW
- Risk per trade: 1% = 100,000 KRW
- Entry price (Buy USD/KRW): 1,350
- Stop-loss: 1,340
- Stop distance: 10 KRW

Position size = 100,000 KRW / 10 KRW = **10,000 USD** (1 mini lot)

**Verification:** If USD/KRW drops from 1,350 to 1,340, loss = 10,000 USD x 10 KRW = 100,000 KRW = exactly 1% of account. Correct.

**Worked example for EUR/USD:**

- Account balance: $5,000
- Risk per trade: 2% = $100
- Entry price (Buy EUR/USD): 1.0850
- Stop-loss: 1.0820
- Stop distance: 30 pips = 0.0030
- Pip value per standard lot: $10

Position size = $100 / (30 pips x $10/pip per standard lot) = $100 / $300 = **0.33 standard lots** = 3.3 mini lots = 33,000 EUR

- Which setups work best

### 6.6 Technical vs Fundamental Analysis — Basics

#### Fundamental Analysis

Analyzing economic data, interest rates, political events, and macro trends to predict currency direction.

**For USD/KRW, key fundamental factors:**
- US-Korea interest rate differential
- Korean trade balance trend
- Global risk sentiment (risk-on/risk-off)
- Oil prices
- Semiconductor cycle
- BOK/Fed policy trajectory

**Approach:** Read economic calendars, follow central bank communications, track trade data. Build a macro view. This works best for **medium to long-term** positioning (weeks to months).

#### Technical Analysis

Analyzing price charts and historical patterns to predict future price movements.

**Common technical tools:**

| Tool | What It Shows | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| **Support/Resistance** | Price levels where buying/selling pressure has historically concentrated | Entry/exit points; e.g., USD/KRW 1,300 has been strong support |
| **Moving Averages (MA)** | Smoothed trend of the price | 50-day MA and 200-day MA crossovers signal trend changes |
| **RSI (Relative Strength Index)** | Whether a pair is overbought (>70) or oversold (<30) | Mean-reversion signals |
| **MACD** | Momentum and trend direction | Trend confirmation |
| **Bollinger Bands** | Volatility range around the moving average | Breakout/squeeze signals |
| **Fibonacci Retracements** | Key pullback levels (38.2%, 50%, 61.8%) | Entry levels during corrections |
| **Candlestick Patterns** | Visual representation of price action | Short-term reversal/continuation signals |

**Approach:** Works best for **short-term** trading (intraday to a few days). Many traders combine fundamental analysis (for direction) with technical analysis (for timing).

### 6.7 Common Beginner Mistakes

1. **Using too much leverage.** Start with 5:1 or 10:1 at most. The fact that 50:1 or 100:1 is available does not mean you should use it.

2. **No stop-loss.** "I'll close it manually if it goes against me" — you won't. Emotions prevent it. Always use automatic stop-losses.

3. **Risking too much per trade.** If you are risking 10% or more of your account on a single trade, you are gambling, not trading.

4. **Trading without a plan.** Before entering, you should know: entry price, stop-loss, take-profit, position size, and the thesis. If you cannot articulate these, do not trade.

5. **Trading too many pairs.** Focus on 1-3 pairs that you understand deeply. For Korean traders, USD/KRW and perhaps EUR/USD or USD/JPY is plenty.

6. **Ignoring the economic calendar.** Getting caught in a position during a major data release (NFP, FOMC) without knowing it is coming is amateur hour.

7. **Revenge trading.** After a loss, taking a bigger trade to recover is the fastest way to destroy an account.

8. **Not keeping a journal.** You cannot improve what you do not measure.

9. **Expecting to get rich quickly.** Professional traders aim for 10-30% annual returns. If someone promises you 100%+ returns, they are lying or taking reckless risks.

10. **Ignoring costs.** Spreads, commissions, and swaps add up. On a mini lot of EUR/USD with a 1-pip spread, your cost is $1 per trade. If you make 20 trades per day, that is $20/day, $400/month, $4,800/year — possibly more than your starting capital.

11. **Trusting "signal providers" or "FX mentors" on social media.** The vast majority are scams or unprofitable traders selling education. If they could consistently make money trading, they would not need to sell signals for $50/month.

12. **Not understanding the product.** Trading USD/KRW without understanding the NDF market, BOK intervention, or Korea's capital controls is like driving blindfolded.

## 7. Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Korean (참고) | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **Ask (Offer)** | 매도호가 | The price at which the market will sell to you (the price you pay to buy). Always higher than the bid. |
| **Base Currency** | 기준통화 | The first currency in a pair. In USD/KRW, USD is the base currency. |
| **Bid** | 매수호가 | The price at which the market will buy from you (the price you receive when selling). |
| **Carry Trade** | 캐리 트레이드 | Borrowing in a low-interest-rate currency and investing in a high-interest-rate currency to earn the interest differential. |
| **Central Bank Intervention** | 중앙은행 외환시장 개입 | When a central bank buys or sells currency to influence the exchange rate. The BOK is known for active intervention. |
| **Cross Pair** | 교차통화쌍 | A currency pair that does not include USD. Example: EUR/JPY, GBP/CHF. |
| **Drawdown** | 드로다운 | The decline from a peak equity value to a trough. Measures how much you have lost from your highest point before recovering. |
| **ECN (Electronic Communication Network)** | - | A type of broker that connects traders directly to liquidity providers, offering tighter spreads and faster execution. |
| **Exotic Pair** | 이종통화쌍 | A currency pair including one major currency and one emerging market currency. USD/KRW is an exotic pair. |
| **Fibonacci Retracement** | 피보나치 되돌림 | Technical tool using key ratios (23.6%, 38.2%, 50%, 61.8%) to identify potential support/resistance levels. |
| **Fixing Rate (매매기준율)** | 매매기준율 | The official benchmark exchange rate set at a specific time. For USD/KRW, it is set at 15:30 KST daily by Seoul Money Brokerage. |
| **Flat (Square)** | 스퀘어 포지션 | Having no open positions. |
| **Floating P&L** | 평가손익 | Unrealized profit or loss on an open position. |
| **FOMC** | - | Federal Open Market Committee — the US Federal Reserve's monetary policy decision-making body. Their meetings are among the most market-moving events globally. |
| **Gap** | 갭 | A discontinuity in price where the market opens significantly higher or lower than the previous close. Common over weekends. |
| **Hedge** | 헤지 | A position taken to offset or reduce risk from another position or exposure. Korean exporters hedge by selling USD forward. |
| **Leverage** | 레버리지 | Using borrowed capital to increase position size. 50:1 leverage means controlling $50 for every $1 of your own money. |
| **Limit Order** | 지정가 주문 | An order to buy or sell at a specified price or better. |
| **Liquidity** | 유동성 | The ability to buy or sell without causing significant price impact. Major pairs (EUR/USD) have high liquidity; exotic pairs (USD/KRW) have lower liquidity. |
| **Long** | 매수 포지션 | Buying a currency pair, expecting the base currency to appreciate. Long USD/KRW = bullish on USD, bearish on KRW. |
| **Lot** | 랏 | Standard unit of trade size. Standard lot = 100,000 units, mini = 10,000, micro = 1,000. |
| **Margin** | 증거금 | The amount of capital required to open and maintain a leveraged position. |
| **Margin Call** | 마진 콜 | A warning or notification that your account equity has fallen below the required margin level. You must deposit more funds or close positions. |
| **Market Maker** | 마켓 메이커 | A broker or bank that provides both buy and sell prices, effectively taking the other side of your trade. |
| **NDF (Non-Deliverable Forward)** | 차액결제선물환 | A forward contract settled in cash (usually USD) rather than physical delivery of the restricted currency. The primary way KRW trades internationally. |
| **Pip** | 핍 | The smallest standard unit of price change. For EUR/USD: 0.0001. For USD/JPY: 0.01. For USD/KRW: varies by platform (often 0.01 or 0.10 KRW). |
| **Position** | 포지션 | An open trade (either long or short). |
| **Quote Currency** | 표시통화 | The second currency in a pair. In USD/KRW, KRW is the quote currency. |
| **Requote** | 재호가 | When a broker rejects your order at the displayed price and offers a new price. Common with market-maker brokers during volatile conditions. |
| **Rollover** | 롤오버 | The process of extending the settlement date of an open position to the next day. The swap fee/credit is applied during rollover. |
| **Short** | 매도 포지션 | Selling a currency pair, expecting the base currency to depreciate. Short USD/KRW = bearish on USD, bullish on KRW. |
| **Slippage** | 슬리피지 | The difference between the expected execution price and the actual execution price. |
| **Spread** | 스프레드 | The difference between the bid and ask price. Your primary transaction cost. |
| **Stop-Loss** | 손절매 주문 | An order to close a position at a specified loss level to limit further losses. |
| **Swap** | 스왑 | The interest rate differential paid or received for holding a position overnight. Also called rollover interest. |
| **Take-Profit** | 익절매 주문 | An order to close a position at a specified profit level to lock in gains. |
| **Technical Analysis** | 기술적 분석 | Analyzing price charts, patterns, and indicators to predict future price movements. |
| **Trailing Stop** | 트레일링 스탑 | A dynamic stop-loss that moves with the price in your favor but stays fixed when the price moves against you. |
| **Volatility** | 변동성 | The degree of price fluctuation. Higher volatility means larger price swings and greater risk/opportunity. |
| **Volume** | 거래량 | The total amount of currency traded during a specific period. Higher volume generally means better liquidity. |
